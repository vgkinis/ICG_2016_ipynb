Atoms nuclei consist of two kinds of particles; protons and neutrons - they roughly have the same mass. Protons are positively charged.

  • Atomic Number Z: number of protons
  • N: The number of neutrons
  • The mass number A is the sum of protons and neutrons (nucleons) and $A = Z+A$

Elements with the same $Z$ but different $A$ are called isotopes.

The Atomic number $Z$ typically determines the chemical properties of an element.

For the light elements the most abundant nuclides lie on the $Z = N$ line and are stable $\left(^{2}_{1}\text{H}_2\,,\, ^{4}_{2}\text{He}_2\,,\, ^{12}_{\,\,6}\text{C}_6 \,,\, ^{14}_{\,\,7}\text{N}_7 \,,\, ^{16}_{\,\,8}\text{O}_8\right)$. A slight deviation from the $Z = N$ line is also possible for stable nuclei $\left(^{17}_{\,\,8}\text{O}_9 \,,\,^{18}_{\,\,8}\text{O}_{10}\right)$. Assymetric nuclei typically have lower natural abundances.

  • Oxygen Triplet: $^{16}\text{O}\,,\,^{17}\text{O}\,,\,^{18}\text{O}:\,0.9976\,,\,0.00038\,,\,0.00205$
  • Hydrogen : $^{1}\text{H}\,,\,^{2}\text{H}:\,0.99985, \,0.00015$

Abundance and Fractionation¶

Isotopic Abundance

Isotopic Abundances are defined as: $R = \frac{\text{abundance of rare isotope}}{\text{abundance of abundant isotope}}$

so we can define $^{18}\text{R(H}_2\text{O}) = \frac{\left[\text{H}_2{}^{18}\text{O}\right]}{\left[\text{H}_2{}^{16}\text{O}\right]}$, $^{2}\text{R(H}_2\text{O}) = \frac{\left[\text{HD}{}^{16}\text{O}\right]}{2\left[\text{H}_2{}^{16}\text{O}\right]}$

This is not an isotope concentration (molar ratio). This would be defined as: $\frac{\left[\text{H}_2{}^{18}\text{O}\right]}{\left[\text{H}_2{}^{18}\text{O}\right]+ \left[\text{H}_2{}^{16}\text{O}\right]}$

Another (not so) common expression that can be find in the literature is the atom%. This is defined as: $\frac{N_X}{N_{tot}}\cdot100\%$, where $N_X$ the number of atoms of the isotope X and $N_{tot}$ the total number of atoms.

Fractionation¶

The various isotopologues of water present very small differences with respect to their chemical and mostly physical properties. This is a phenomenon called isotopic fractionation. Typical examples of processes where isotopic fractionation can be observed are the transitions between states (liquid to vapor, solid to liquid etc.).

Reasons for isotopic fractionation¶

  • Kinetics Temperature determines the kinetic energy of a molecule as $kT \sim \frac{1}{2}mv^2$. As a result, at the same $T$ the heavier isotopologues will present lower velocities on average. As a result diffusion velocities and collision frequencies will be lower for the heavier isotopologues.

  • Binding Energies Heavier isotopologues generally (not always!) present higher binding energies thus "sitting" deeper in the energy well as seen in the picture below.

Practically this results in:

  • Light isotopologues react faster in chemical reactions.
  • Light isotopologues tend to have higher vapor pressures and thus evaporate easier.
  • At high temperatures fractionation effects diminish.

Fractionation Factors - Equilibrium, Diffusion, Kinetic¶

Equilibrium Fractionation¶

Lets assume the following reaction where ${}^{0}\text{X}$ and ${}^{1}\text{X}$ are two different isotopes of the element X.

$\text{A}{}^{0}\text{X} + \text{B}{}^{1}\text{X} \rightleftharpoons \text{A}{}^{1}\text{X} + \text{B}{}^{0}\text{X}$

then the equilibrium constant is $K_X(T) = \frac{\left[\text{A}{}^{1}\text{X}\right]\left[ \text{B}{}^{0}\text{X} \right] }{\left[ \text{A}{}^{0}\text{X} \right] \left[ \text{B}{}^{1} \text{X} \right]} = \frac{R_{AX}}{R_{BX}} = \alpha_{AX/BX}$

Here $\alpha_{AX/BX} $ is called the fractionation factor. A very relevant reaction for ice core studies is the under equilibrium evaporation/condensation process of the water molecule:

$ \text{H}_2{}^{16}\text{O}_{vap} + \text{H}_2{}^{18}\text{O}_{liq} \leftrightharpoons \text{H}_2{}^{16}\text{O}_{liq} + \text{H}_2{}^{18}\text{O}_{vap} $

For which the fractionation factor of the ${}^{18}\text{O}$ will be: $K(T) = \frac{\left[\text{H}_2{}^{18}\text{O}_{vap}\right]\left[ \text{H}_2{}^{16}\text{O}_{liq} \right] }{\left[ \text{H}_2{}^{16}\text{O}_{vap} \right] \left[ \text{H}_2{}^{18} \text{O}_{liq} \right]} = \frac{{}^{18}R_{vap}}{{}^{18}R_{liq}} = {}^{18}\alpha_{v/l}$

${}^{18}\alpha_{v/l}$ has a value vey close to 1 (remember that especially at higher temperatures the fractionation effects are of very small magnitude). For convenience we also define the fractionation ${}^{18}\varepsilon_{v/l} = {}^{18}\alpha_{v/l} - 1$ that has a value very close to zero and typically it is expressed in per mil $\text{\textperthousand$
